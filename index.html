The real cost of bad AI adoption

  • Your team tried it for a week, decided it “wasn’t useful,” and went back to doing everything manually.
  • The people who could benefit most — operations, compliance, project leads — aren’t touching it.
  • New hires are getting onboarded the slow way while the tools that could cut ramp time in half sit idle.
  • Meanwhile, your competitors’ teams are shipping faster, writing better, and automating the grunt work yours still does by hand.
  • Renewal is coming. Leadership wants to see ROI. You don’t have a number to show them.

The technology works. The adoption didn’t. That’s not an IT problem — it’s a people problem. And it’s fixable.

Why me

AI adoption is a behavior change problem, not a technology problem. Tools don’t transform companies — people using tools differently do. That belief shapes everything about how this practice works.

It also means the person working with your team should understand the technology at a deep level — not just how to prompt it, but how it actually works, where it breaks, and what it can’t do. A SaaS product shipped on Meta’s SAM 3. A multi-agent outreach system running on Claude with a custom SQLite backend and automated enrichment. A personal RAG library built from scratch. This isn’t theory — it’s daily practice.

And the person working with your team should know how to teach. 8,500+ hours of live instruction — engineers, finance professionals, students, government employees across different countries, every proficiency level. The kind of experience where you learn to read a room, adjust on the fly, and meet people exactly where they are.

Most AI consultants are either engineers who can’t teach, or trainers who don’t understand the technology. The gap between those two is where adoption fails — and where this practice lives.
